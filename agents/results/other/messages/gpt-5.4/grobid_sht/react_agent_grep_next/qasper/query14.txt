3.2.1 Direction-and Distance-Aware Attention
Inspired by the success of BiLSTM in NER tasks, we consider what properties the Transformer lacks compared to BiLSTM-based models. One observation is that BiLSTM can discriminatively collect the context information of a token from its left and right sides. But it is not easy for the Transformer to distinguish which side the context information comes from. Although the dot product between two sinusoidal position embeddings is able to reflect their distance, it lacks directionality and this property will be broken by the vanilla Transformer attention. They incorporate it by using relative positional encodings \(R_{t-j}\) based on the offset between target token \(t\) and context token \(j\), and adding terms involving \(R_{t-j}\) directly into the attention score: \(A^{rel}_{t,j}=Q_tK_j^T+Q_tR_{t-j}^T+uK_j^T+vR_{t-j}^T\). Because \(R_{t-j}\) uses sinusoidal functions of the relative offset, forward and backward positions differ in the sine terms while preserving distance information, allowing attention to distinguish both direction and relative distance.
